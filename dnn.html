What is a neuron?

Simply put, a human brain is a real life complex example of Deep Neural Networks work.

Signals in the brain are sent between neurons via synapses and depending on which neuron is activated by a signal, a different outcome is achieved.

A brain's neural network consists of trillions of synapses with millions of signals firing simultaneously to send and receive information. This allows humans to think fast and react fast.

Image showing how a neuron's output is based on weight i.e. 0 weight for no output, 1 for output.

In a similar sense, an Artificial Neural Network sends signals between nodes (Neurons) to provide a valued output.

Density and Layers

The density of a Neural Network can usually reflect the complexity of a task being processed.
Depending on the task at hand, the amount of layers between an input to a Neural Network identifying a correct output can vary.

CNN

Convolutional Neural Networks (ConvNet) are primarily used for image recognition due to its layout of neurons being efficient for pattern recognition.

An image can be identified by the human brain by identifying patterns i.e. the shape of a shoe gives away the fact that something is in fact a shoe.


This is different with machine learning as we have to teach an Artificial Neural Network to detect these patterns as our brain has perfected over many years of experience.

How it works is that it slices images and flattens them into matrixes of numbers, which are then filtered through multiple convolutional layers of neurons to detect patterns.
Generally... It is easier to work with Greyscale images as that way the neural network does not have to take colour into account.
In real life situations, colour is just as important to perfectly classify an object.

This can apply to many real life objects, creatures or even people to recognise who or what something is based on an image.
